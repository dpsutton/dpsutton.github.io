1 Definitions

1.1 Battery

A defendant is liable for battery when he intentionally causes a harmful or offensive contact with the plaintiff's person.

1.2 Assaullt

A defendant is liable for assault when she intentionally causes a reasonable apprehension in the plaintiff of an immediate harmful or offensive contact to the plaintiff's person.

1.3 IIED

A defendant is liable for IIED when he acts in an extreme and outrageous manner to intentionally cause the plaintiff emotional distress, and the plaintiff suffers severe emotional distress.

1.4 Consent

A plaintiff consents when he agrees to the defendant's conduct

1.5 Intent

"In order that an act may be done with the intention of bringing about a harmful or offensive contact or an apprehension thereof to a particular person, … the act must be done for the purpose of causing the contact or apprehension or with knowledge on the part of the actor that such contact or apprehension is substantially certian to be produced"

From the restatement of torts quoted in Garratt v Dailey

To qualify it from negligence same case and source,

"It is not enough that the act itself is intentionally done and this, even though the actor realizes or should realize that it contains a very grave risk of bringing about the contact or apprehension. Such realization may make the actor's conduct negligent or even reckless but unless he realizes that to a substantial certainty, the contact or apprehension will result, the actor has not that intention whic his necessary to make him liable undre the rule stated in this section"

2 intent

2.1 garratt v dailey

2.1.1 bad rule

wilful or unlawful purpose; any intent to injure the plaintiff or any intent to bring about any unauthoried or offensive contact with her person

did not have purpose intent or design to perform a prank or to effect an assault and battery upon the person of the plaintiff.

2.1.2 good rule

Intentional infliction of a harmful bodily contact upon another (loose)

2.1.3 character of actor's intention

In order than an act may be done with the intention of bringing about a harmful or offensive contact or an apprehension thereof to a particular person, either the other or a third person, the act must be done for the purpose of causing the contact or apprehension or with knowledge on the part of the actor that such contact or apprehension is substantially certain to be produced.

Commentary of the above: It is not enough that the act itself is intentionally done and this, even though the actor realizes or should realize that it contains a very grave risk of bringing about the contact or apprehension. Such realization may make the actor's conduct negligent or even reckless but unless he realizes that to a substantial certainty, the contact or apprehension will result, the actor has not that intention which is necessary to make him liabile under the rule stated in this section.

2.1.4 procedural history

Boy prevails, no intent to injure. Clarify rule for intent (intent to cause the contact or knowledge that contact will occur with substantial certainty) and goes back and find in favor of Ruth Garratt.

2.2 spivey v battaglia

2.2.1 bad rule

Where a reasonable man would believe that a particular result was substantially certain to follow, he will be held in the eyes of the law as though he had intended it.

It cannot be said that a reasonable man in this defendant's position would believe that the bizarre results herein were "substantially certain" to follow.

2.3 ranson v kitner

mistake is not an accident. Mistakenly shoot neighbors dog thinking it was a wolf. Judgement in favor of dog owners.

Intentional tort, regardless of motives. Not an accident but a mistake.

2.4 mcguire v almy

The extent to which an insane person is liabile for torts has not been fully defined in this commonwealth.

An insane person may not be able to form malice

"the ability of the defendant to understand the particular act in question or to make a reasoned decision with respect to it, although it is sometimes said that an insane person is not liable for torts requiring malice of which he is incapable."

Contrasts to "general modern theory that liability in tort should rest upon fault".

"In so far as a particular intent would be necessary in order to render a normal person liable, the insane person, in order to be liable, must have been capable of entertaining that same intent and must have entertained it in fact. But the law will not inquire further into his peculiar mental condition with a view to excusing him if it hsould appear that delusion or other consequences of his affliction has caused him to entertain that intent or that a normal person would not have entertained it."

2.5 talmage v smith

Throws stick at a boy on his shed, missed him and hit plaintiff resulting in total loss of sight. Intent to him the other boy.

"The right of the plaintiff to recover was made to depend upon the intention on the part of the defendant to hit somebody, and to inflect an unwarranted injury upon some on. Under these circumstances, the fact that the injury resulted to another than was intended does not relieve the defendant from responsibility".

Judment affirmed

2.5.1 transfer of intent

five torts that fall within the trespass writ:

  1. battery
  2. assault
  3. false imprisonment
  4. trespass to land
  5. trespass to chattels

"When the defendant intends any one of the five, and accomplishes any one of the five, the doctrine applies and the defendant is liable, even if the plaintiff was not the intended target".

"On the other hand, when either the tort intended or the one accomplished does not fall within the trespass action, the doctrine does not apply".

3 battery

3.1 cole v turner

  1. That the least touching of another in anger is a battery
  2. If two or more meet in a narrow passage, and without any violence or design of harm, the one touches the other gently it will be no battery.
  3. If any of them use violence against the other, to force his way in a rude inordinate manner, it is a battery; or any struggle about the passage, to that degree as may do hurt, it is a battery.

3.2 wallace v rosen

3.2.1 Battery Rule

A battery is the knowing or intentional touching of one person by another in a rude, insolent or angry manner.

Any touching, however slight, may constitute an assault and battery.

3.2.2 Qualifications

"In a loose and general sense, the meaning of "intent" is easy to grasp. As Holmes observed, eve na dog knows the difference between being tripped over and being kicked. This is also the key distinction between two major divisions of legal liability–negligence and intentional torts…"

"the mere knowledge and appreciation of risk–something short of substantial certainty–is not intent. … The line has to be drawn by the courts at the point where the known danger ceases to be only a foreseeable risk which a reasonable person would avoid, and becomes in the mind of the actor a substantial certainty."

"The intent with which tort liability is concerned is not necessarily a hostile intent or a desire to do any harm. Rather it is an intent to bring about a result which will invade the interest of aonther in a way that the law forbigs".

3.2.3 Rule

For the touch which caused the fall, "the evidence had to support an inference not only that Rosen intentionally touched Wallace, but that she did so in a rude, insolent or angry manner".

  1. Busy World:

    "Absent expression to the contrary, consent is assumed to all those ordinary contacts which are customary and reasonably necessary to the common intercourse of life, such as a tap on the shoulder to attract attention, a friendly grasp of the arm, or a casual jostling to make a passage…"

  2. Standard

    "Unless the defendant has special reason to believe that more or less will be permitted by the individual plaintiff, the test is what would be offensive to an ordinary person not unduly sensitive as to personal dignity"

3.2.4 Holding

Teacher is not liable for damages from the fall as they were in a "crowded world" situation and she was getting her attention. "The actions cannot be said to be a rude, insolent or angry touching".

3.3 restatement of torts

Restatement (Second) of Torts (1965)

3.3.1 Battery: Harmful Contact

An actor is subject to liability to another for battery if

  1. he acts intending to cause a harmful or offensive contact with the person of the other or a third person, or an imminent apprehension of such a contact, and
  2. a harmful contact with the person of the other directly or indirectly results.

3.3.2 Battery: Offensive Contact

  1. An actor is subject to liability to another for battery if
    1. he acts intending to cause a harmful or offensive contact with the person of the other or a third person, or an imminent apprehension of such a contact, and
    2. an offensive contact with the person of the other directly or indirectly results
  2. An act which is not done with the intention stated in Subsection (1,1) does not make the actor liabile to the other for a mere offensive contact with the other's person although the act involves an unreasonable risk of inflicting it and, therefore, would be negligent or reckless if the risk threatened bodily harm.

3.4 fisher v carrousel motor hotel

"Plaintiff was not actually touched, and was in no apprehension of physical injry; but he was highly embarrassed and hurt by the conduct in the presence of his associates." Jury returns verdict in his favor which judge throws out.

3.4.1 Rule

"The intentional snatching of an object from one's hand is as clearly an offensive invasion of his person as would be an actual contact with the body. 'To constitute an assault and battery, it is not necessary to touch the plaintiff's body or even his clothing; knocking or snatchign anything from plaintiff's hand or touching anythign connected with his person, when done in an offensive manner, is sufficient'".

3.4.2 Rationale

From the restatement

"Since the essence of th plaintiff's grievance consists in the offense to the dignity involved in the npermitted and intentional invasion of the inviolability of his person and not in any physical harm done to his body, it is not necessary that the plaintiff's actual body be disturbed. Unpermitted and intentional contacts with anything so connected with the body as to be customarily regarded as part of the other's person and therefore partaking of its inviolability is actionable as an offensive contact ith his person."

4 assault

4.1 I de S et ux v W de S

4.1.1 Rule

"There is harm done and a trespass for which he shall recover damages since he made an assault upon the woman, as has been found, although he did no othe harm. … And so note that for an assault a man shall recover damages, etc."

4.1.2 Policy

Wielding a hatchet at someone certainly strikes us as injustice; for this to be unactionable is absurd, even if there are no damages.

4.2 Western Union Telegraph Co v Hill

4.2.1 Rule

"To constitute an actionable assault there must be an intentional, unlawful, offer to touch the person of another in a rude or angry manner under such circumstances as to create in the mind of the part alleging the assault a well-founded fear of an imminent battery, coupled with the apparent present ability to effectuate the attempt, if not prevented".

4.2.2 Takeaway

Focuses on apparent possibility. And apparent to the person receiving the assault.

Defense was that he was physically prevented from making contact; but hold that he could have taken steps to make the contact possible. Either way, "as to create in the mind of the part alleging the assault a well-founded fear of an imminent battery"

5 Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

5.1 State Rubbish Collectors Ass'n v. Siliznoff

I absolutely love this case.

"Plaintiff contends that the evidence does not establish an assault against defendant because the threats made all related to action that might take place in the future; that neither Andikian onr members of the board of directors threatened immediate physical harm to defendant."

5.1.1 Rule

"A cause of action is established when it is shown that one, in the absence of any privilege, intetionally subjects another to the mental suffering incident to seroius threats to his physical well-being, whether or not the threats are made under such circumstances as to constitute a technical assault".

"One who, without a privilige to do so, intentionally causes severe emotional distress to another is liable (a) for such emotional distress, and (b) for bodily harm resulting from it."

The only intentional tort that requires harm.

5.1.2 Reasoning

Previously could collect for emotional distress but not as a cause only incidental to another tort.

5.1.3 Policy

"No reason or policy requires such an actor to be protected from the liability which usually attaches to the willful wrongdoer whose efforts are successful"

6 consent

6.1 O'Brien v. Cunard S.S. Co.

"the question is whether there was any evidence that the surgeon used force upon the plaintiff against her will. … If the plaintiff's behavior was such as to indicate consent on her part, he was justified in his act, whatever her unexpressed feelings may have been. In determing whether she consented, he could be guided only by her overt act and the manifestatoins of her feelings."

Made no protestations at the time, offered her arm for vaccination, signs everywhere, line of women to get vaccinated, used the cert at the port. Evidence points to consent.

6.2 Hackbart v. Cincinnati Bengals, Inc.

Definitely an intentional tort, question is is it consented to by playing football. Lower court ruled that it was since football is a special kind of "warfare" (crazy terminology).

"[D]efendant .. admitted that the blow which had been struck was not accidental, that it was intentionally administered, the trial court ruled as a matter of law that the game of professional football is basically a business which is violent in nature, and that the available sanctions are imposition of penalties and expulsion from the game. Notice was taken of the fact that many fouls are overlooked; that the game is played in an emotional and noisy environment; and that incidents such as that here complained of are not unusual."

6.2.1 Holding

Lower court dismissed ruling in favor of the Bengals. Sends back to hold to just the evidence.

6.2.2 Reasoning

  • "the very conduct which was present here is expressly prohibited by the rule[s]"
  • general customs of football do not approve the intentional punching or striking of others… "establishing reasonable boundaries"

"In sum, having concluded that the trial court did not limit the case to a trial of the evidence bearing on defendant's liability but rather determined that as a matter of social policy the game was so violent and unlawful that valid lines could not be drawn, we take the view that this was not a proper issue for determination and that plaintiff was entitled to have the case tried on an assessment of his rights and whether they had been violated".
